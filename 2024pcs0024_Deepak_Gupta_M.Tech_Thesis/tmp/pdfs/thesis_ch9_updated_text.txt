9 Conclusion and Future Work
9.1

Summary of the Thesis

The argument the thesis has been making is, in one line, that Byzantine-resilient
privacy-preserving federated learning does not actually need an external auditor
to decrypt cluster-level structure. FIP-FL swaps that trust anchor for a functional
inner product taken between each encrypted client update and a momentumtracked trusted reference direction. The single scalar that the inner product
produces is what feeds the two-stage geometric audit — the direction filter,
which catches label-flip style reversals, and the magnitude window, which catches
updates that are either too weak or too aggressive.
The proposed approach is implemented through a simple pipeline. Paillier encryption is used at the cryptographic layer, the verifier computes client
scores homomorphically, the server aggregates only the accepted encrypted updates, and the reference direction is updated after each round using momentum.
The theoretical analysis lines up with this design — scalar-gradient privacy, a
geometric admissibility condition for any malicious update, and a monotone
loss-decrease under L-smoothness. Empirically, FIP-FL ends up detecting every malicious-client configuration in the completed grid, lifts MNIST non-IID
target-class accuracy from roughly 7% in the undefended baseline to 97.69% at a
50% malicious-client rate, and replaces the auditor’s O(d3 ) covariance-inversion
step with a verification stage that parallelises to a 13× wall-clock speed-up in
Phase II.

Limitations and Future Work

FIP-FL was designed around a single reference direction per round. That choice
is what keeps verification linear and is part of why the method is so cheap to
run; but in a federation that is unusually heterogeneous, the honest clients may
genuinely have several legitimate descent directions, and a single reference can
under-admit the weakly-aligned but honest ones. The natural fix is a multi(t)
reference cone {Rk }K
k=1 , but introducing several references brings its own risk —
an adversary could try to live in the overlap region between two cones — and
any future version of this work would have to add concrete safeguards against
that.
The main limitations come from the experimental setting. This thesis evaluates FIP-FL using softmax logistic-regression models, parameter dimensions in
the low thousands, a fixed set of clients, and one attack type per run. Extending
the method to deep networks will likely require packed homomorphic encryption
schemes such as BFV or CKKS [12]. A more realistic setting would also need to
handle client churn, changing data distributions, adaptive attackers, and mixed
attacks such as label flipping combined with update scaling.
Another limitation is the initial reference direction. In the current design,
it is obtained from a small public dataset. This works in the experiments,
but it assumes that such data are available. A stronger first-round method
could compute the reference from robust aggregate statistics over the clients
themselves, while still avoiding decryption of individual gradients. After this,
FIP-FL should be tested on real laptop, mobile, and edge clients to study the
trade-off between encryption cost, communication cost, and model utility in
practical PPFL settings.
