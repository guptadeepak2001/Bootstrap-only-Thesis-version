Poster Abstract: Auditor-Free Byzantine-Resilient
Federated Learning via Functional Inner Products
Mahendra Kumar Gurve, Deepak Gupta, Nitin∗ and Yamuna Prasad
Dept. of CSE, Indian Institute of Technology Jammu, India;{mahendra.gurve, 2024pcs0024, yamuna.prasad}@iitjammu.ac.in
∗ Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Cincinnati, USA; nitinfu@ucmail.uc.edu

Abstract—Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL) is
inherently vulnerable to Byzantine model poisoning, particularly
when encrypted gradients prevent direct inspection. Existing
defenses are often based on auditor-based architectures, introducing additional computational and communication overhead,
as well as trust assumptions. This paper proposes an auditorfree Byzantine-resilient PPFL framework grounded in Functional
Inner Product (FIP) geometry. By evaluating gradient alignment
with a trusted reference direction and applying robust statistical
thresholding, malicious updates are detected without decrypting
raw gradients. Experimental results on MNIST under non-IID
settings demonstrate superior robustness against targeted and
untargeted poisoning attacks, achieving state-of-the-art targeted
accuracy without external auditing.
Index Terms—Federated Learning, Privacy-Preserving, Functional Inner Product, Model Poisoning.

I. I NTRODUCTION
Federated Learning (FL) is a scalable distributed optimization framework that enables collaborative model training without exposing raw data. By exchanging model updates instead of local datasets, FL enhances data locality
and mitigates privacy risks. However, FL remains inherently
vulnerable to model poisoning attacks, where Byzantine or
adversarial clients manipulate gradients to disrupt convergence
or induce targeted misclassification. This threat is further
amplified in Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL),
where encrypted gradients prevent direct inspection and limit
conventional anomaly detection.
Existing defenses rely on robust aggregation rules, statistical
filtering, or auditor-based [1] architectures that evaluate gradient consistency prior to aggregation. Although auditor-centric
frameworks improve robustness through clustering and distributional modeling, they introduce considerable computational
and communication overhead. Furthermore, incorporating a
trusted auditing entity increases cryptographic complexity,
latency, and scalability as well as single-point-of-failure concerns in large-scale heterogeneous deployments.
To address these challenges, this research proposes an
auditor-free Byzantine-resilient federated learning framework
based on functional inner product analysis. It uses gradient
alignment in a functional space to detect adversarial updates
without external auditing, preserving privacy, reducing overhead, and ensuring robust convergence.
Contributions: 1: Auditor-free Byzantine-resilient FL with
lightweight encrypted gradient alignment using functional

inner product geometry. 2: Framework validated to improve
robustness and scalability over auditor-based methods.

III. E XPERIMENT & R ESULTS
A. Experimental Setup
This study strictly follows the experimental setup of the
base paper [1] for fair benchmarking, using logistic regression
on MNIST under non-IID settings. The proposed framework
operates on homomorphically encrypted gradients and is evaluated against both targeted and untargeted poisoning attacks.
Furthermore, performance is measured using Target Accuracy
(Tacc ), Other Label Accuracy (Oacc ), and Overall Accuracy
(ACC) to quantify attack success and defense robustness.

Table I shows Non-IID results (attack rate = 50%). Without
defense, targeted attacks reduce Tacc to 0.07 despite moderate
overall accuracy. With the proposed defense, Tacc improves to
0.78 while maintaining high Other Label Accuracy (0.97) and
overall accuracy (0.97), demonstrating effective mitigation.

IV. C ONCLUSION
This work presents an auditor-free, Byzantine-resilient federated learning framework that leverages functional inner
product geometry and robust statistical thresholding to detect
malicious updates in encrypted gradients. Experiments on
MNIST demonstrate strong robustness against both targeted
and untargeted poisoning attacks, maintaining high accuracy
while reducing computational and communication overhead.
Future work will evaluate the framework on IID settings and
a wider variety of datasets to further validate its scalability
and effectiveness.
