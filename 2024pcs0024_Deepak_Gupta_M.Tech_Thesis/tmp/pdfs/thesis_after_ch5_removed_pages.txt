Abstract
Federated Learning, or FL, lets many clients jointly fit one model without
sending their raw data anywhere. To tighten the privacy story, recent work
wraps every client update in an additive homomorphic cipher, giving the family
known as Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL). The catch is that the
same cipher which keeps a curious server out of the gradients also keeps the
server from telling honest clients apart from bad ones. A Byzantine client can
simply flip its labels and silently push the global model in a wrong direction,
pulling accuracy down or planting a back-door on one chosen class.
Most defences here bring in an auditor: a trusted third party that decrypts
cluster-level statistics of the incoming updates and screens out the bad ones with
distributional models. They work, but at a price. The auditor is itself a new
trust anchor, the very thing PPFL was meant to remove. Decrypting cluster
summaries also leaks structural information about the gradients, and the cubic
covariance step makes cost grow much faster than d. A defence that is at once
fully encrypted, free of any extra trust anchor, and linear in d has so far been
missing.
Functional Inner Product–Federated Learning, the framework developed in
this thesis, is built around one idea: the verifier never needs to see a raw gradient.
Working entirely under Paillier encryption, it computes one projection score
per client: the Functional Inner Product (FIP) of that client’s update against a
momentum-tracked reference direction. Once the FIP scalar is decrypted, two
checks are applied: a direction filter, then a median–MAD magnitude window.
The chapters that follow analyse this protocol along three axes: how much a
scalar score actually reveals about the gradient, what region of update-space
is still open to an attacker, and whether descent is preserved when the loss is
L-smooth.
I built the framework end-to-end and ran it on MNIST and KDDCup99,
with both IID and non-IID client splits, and with targeted and untargeted
label-flipping attacks at malicious-client rates up to fifty percent. Across this
grid, FIP-FL stays robust without any external auditor.

1 Introduction
1.1

Background and Context

Federated Learning, FL for short, was put forward by McMahan and his coauthors [2] as a way of training a single global model without ever pulling the
raw data into one place. The clients keep their samples on their own devices
and send back only a gradient or a parameter update. That property is what
makes the FL recipe useful for things like mobile keyboards, medical records,
fraud-detection feeds, and anywhere else that the data is sensitive and naturally
distributed.
But keeping the data local is, by itself, not really enough. The shared gradients
can still leak training samples [3], leak membership, or leak distributional traits
of the underlying records. The line of work that hardens this channel is called
Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning, or PPFL. The PPFL literature usually
builds on three main mechanisms: differential privacy, secure aggregation, and
homomorphic encryption. This thesis follows the homomorphic-encryption route.
More precisely, it uses additive homomorphic encryption, which allows the
server to aggregate encrypted client updates while avoiding direct access to any
individual client’s gradient.

1.2

The Research Gap

Encryption improves privacy in federated learning, but it also limits the information available to the server for Byzantine-robust aggregation. A malicious client
may corrupt its local labels, amplify its model update, or submit a deliberately
crafted poisoning vector. Under encryption, however, the server observes only
1

encrypted updates, making such behaviour difficult to distinguish from honest
participation. This creates a limitation for existing robust aggregation methods
such as Krum [4], coordinate-wise median, trimmed-mean [5], and Bulyan [6],
since these methods are designed under the assumption that client updates are
visible in plaintext. A similar issue appears in detection-based defences such
as Auror [7], FL-Trust [8], and PEFL [9], which either require direct access to
gradients or depend on auxiliary information that may weaken the intended
privacy guarantee.
The most relevant baseline that does work over encrypted gradients is the
auditor-based scheme of Yazdinejad et al. [1]. It is effective. But it depends on a
trusted auditor, it decrypts cluster-level summaries to make decisions, it carries
an O(d3 ) covariance-inversion cost, and under non-IID partitions it exposes
structural population information. The piece that is genuinely missing in the
literature, then, is a defence that is at once auditor-free, robust, linear in d, and
still usable when the federation is realistically heterogeneous.

1.3

Research Objectives and Contributions

What this thesis sets out to do is design, analyse, and evaluate FIP-FL —
a Byzantine-resilient PPFL framework in which no auditor is involved. The
verifier looks at each encrypted client update through a single functional inner
product (FIP) against a trusted reference direction; only one scalar per client
per round ever needs to be decrypted. That scalar is then put through a twostage geometric audit, namely a direction filter followed by a median-and-MAD
magnitude window.
The main contributions of the thesis are the following.
1. An auditor-free PPFL architecture. FIP-FL drops the external auditor
entirely and does its verification straight on the ciphertexts, using a scalar
FIP score.
2. A two-stage geometric audit. The direction stage discards the updates
that point the wrong way, and the magnitude stage discards the ones that
are either too weak or too aggressive even when the direction looks right.
3. Formal guarantees. The thesis proves scalar-gradient privacy, gives the

L-smoothness, and shows lower verifier complexity than the auditor-based

4. Empirical validation. FIP-FL is run on MNIST and on KDDCup99,

5. Engineering efficiency. The verifier ends up running in O(N d+N log N ),

2.2

IID and Non-IID Federations

Most analyses of FedAvg assume that client data are IID samples from the same
distribution. In practice, this is rarely true. Users, hospitals, and network sites
often differ in both label distribution and feature patterns. In this thesis, I
consider label skew, where each client receives only a selected subset of labels
with controlled overlap across clients. This makes the local objectives (Fi ) differ
5

from the global objective (F ), so even honest gradients may point in different
directions. A defence must therefore detect malicious updates without treating
normal non-IID variation as an attack.

2.3

Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning

Plain gradients can still reveal private information. A server with access to
them may reconstruct training samples, infer membership, or learn distributional
properties of the data [3]. PPFL reduces this leakage mainly through differential
privacy, secure aggregation, and homomorphic encryption. Differential privacy
clips updates and adds noise, but this can reduce accuracy and may blur the
difference between honest noise and adversarial changes. Secure aggregation [10]
hides individual updates inside a final sum, which protects privacy but prevents
filtering a bad client before aggregation. Homomorphic encryption keeps updates
encrypted while allowing limited computation. Paillier [11] supports encrypted
addition and plaintext scalar multiplication, while BFV and CKKS [12] support
richer operations at higher cost.
For FIP-FL, I use Paillier because the required verification is linear. The
audit scores each encrypted update against a plaintext reference direction using
an inner product. This avoids differential-privacy noise and the coordination
usually required in secure aggregation, while keeping the encrypted computation
linear in the model dimension.

2.4

Poisoning Attack Taxonomy

A Byzantine client can submit any syntactically valid update [13]. This thesis
focuses on two label-flipping attacks. In the untargeted attack, every local label
is shifted by a fixed cyclic rule to reduce overall accuracy. In the targeted attack,
only one source class is mapped to one target class, so the attacker harms a
specific prediction while leaving the rest of the task mostly unaffected. The
experiments do not include sign flipping, scaling attacks, model replacement,
backdoor attacks [14], or collusion. Still, these attacks motivate the direction
and magnitude checks used in the FIP-FL audit.

3 Literature Survey
3.1

Robust Aggregation Rules

One group of defences keeps the FedAvg structure but replaces the mean with
a more robust estimator. Krum and Multi-Krum [4] select updates that are
close to many other updates, but they require plaintext pairwise distances and
have a cost of O(N 2 d). Coordinate-wise median and trimmed-mean [5] are
cheaper, with cost O(N d log N ), but their guarantees are weaker when client
data are non-IID. Under label skew, even honest updates can vary strongly across
coordinates, which makes malicious deviation harder to separate from natural
heterogeneity.

3.2

Detection-Based Defences

Detection-based methods try to identify suspicious clients before aggregation.
However, many of them assume that the server can inspect plaintext gradients. PEFL [9] performs correlation-based scoring in an encrypted setting,
but its robustness becomes limited under high attack rates and non-IID partitions [1]. FoolsGold [15] detects Sybil behaviour using historical similarity,
while ShieldFL [16] uses a helper-assisted secure protocol for encrypted-gradient
detection.
Overall, stronger detection usually requires plaintext access, an additional
trusted party, or higher communication cost. FIP-FL targets this gap by keeping
client updates encrypted, avoiding a new trust entity, and revealing only one
scalar score per client.

Auditor-Based PPFL Baseline

The main baseline for this thesis is the auditor-based method of Yazdinejad
et al. [1]. Their system adds a trusted auditor between the clients and the
aggregation server. The auditor decrypts cluster-level summaries, fits a Gaussian
mixture model on a low-dimensional projection, and uses Mahalanobis distance
to remove outliers. The remaining updates are then sent for homomorphic
aggregation.
The method reports strong results, including about 96.5% target-class accuracy on MNIST non-IID under a 50% targeted label-flipping attack. However,
its limitations are structural. It depends on a trusted auditor, requires covariance inversion with cost O(d3 ), and exposes cluster statistics that may reveal
information about the client population. FIP-FL is designed to reduce these
weaknesses.

3.4

Positioning of FIP-FL

FIP-FL differs from prior work in four main ways. First, individual client updates
remain encrypted. Second, the method does not introduce an external auditor
or helper. Third, it replaces distributional clustering with a geometric alignment
check against a trusted reference direction. Fourth, the verification cost remains
linear in the model dimension d. In this way, FIP-FL aims to combine privacy
preservation with lightweight Byzantine screening.

an encoded update coordinate by an encoded reference coordinate, so once
decrypted it has to be divided by s2 to land back on the right scale.

4.5

System and Threat Model

The FIP-FL protocol has four roles. The clients submit encrypted gradients.
The Aggregation Server takes whatever ciphertexts the audit has accepted and
broadcasts the new model. The FIP Verifier is the role that actually computes
the scalar FIP scores and runs the two-stage check. The Trusted Key Authority
(TKA) generates the Paillier keys and is allowed to decrypt only those scalar
scores and aggregates that the protocol explicitly asks it to. The Verifier may be
co-located with the server in practice; in either case, no external auditor enters
the trust model.

The adversary in this thesis controls up to f < N/2 Byzantine clients. Such
an adversary is free to relabel its own data, hand in any syntactically valid
ciphertext it likes, coordinate with other malicious clients, and listen in on every
public broadcast. The empirical part instantiates this model with targeted and
untargeted label flipping, at malicious rates f /N ∈ {10%, 20%, 30%, 50%}.

5.1

System Architecture

FIP-FL is built around four logical roles: the N clients, an Aggregation Server,
a FIP Verifier, and a Trusted Key Authority (or TKA). In a single round, the
clients put their Paillier ciphertexts on the wire, the Verifier turns each ciphertext
into one alignment score, only the accepted ciphertexts make it to the aggregation
server, and the TKA touches at most scalar scores or aggregate updates — never
an individual plaintext gradient.
This way of cutting the system keeps each role’s responsibility narrow. The
clients train locally and encrypt; the Verifier scores and filters; the server averages
whatever survives the audit; and the TKA does only the decryptions that the
protocol explicitly asks for. The Verifier itself is allowed to look at the encrypted
updates and the plaintext reference direction R(t) , but it is never given an
individual plaintext gradient. The TKA, on the other side, decrypts the scalar
FIP scores and the aggregate updates, but it does not see the audit logic at all.

6.2

Data Partitioning

Each dataset is sliced into N = 10 disjoint client shards, and I run the experiments
under two partition regimes. In the IID regime the training set is shuffled once
and dealt out into equal-sized shards, so each client ends up with roughly
|Dtrain |/N samples and the expected label histogram on every shard matches the
global one.
For the non-IID regime I use label skew. With a C-class task, client i gets a
primary label set Li ⊂ {0, 1, . . . , C − 1} of size ⌈C/2⌉, and the overlap between
successive clients is ⌊C/4⌋. The actual sample assignment then follows an 80/20
split: 80% of a client’s data is drawn from its primary labels and the remaining
20% is drawn from the full training set, which keeps a thin slice of every class
on every shard. This mirrors the SenSys 2026 evaluation protocol and is close
to the non-IID setting of Yazdinejad et al. [1]. The point of this design is
that it is difficult on purpose — honest clients can disagree about the descent
direction without being malicious, and the audit therefore has to tolerate that
heterogeneity rather than treat collinearity as the only signal of honesty.

6.5

Implementation and Hardware

The whole stack is written in Python 3. Linear algebra is handled by NumPy,
the dataset loaders and feature pipelines are taken from scikit-learn, and
the Paillier encryption layer is the phe library at a 1024-bit modulus. The FIP
Verifier and the Aggregation Server live as two separate Python processes that
talk to each other through in-memory queues, and the N clients are simulated as
worker processes around them. Homomorphic encryption and the homomorphic
inner-product loop are both parallelised through multiprocessing.
Unless something else is specified explicitly, the runs use R = 300 rounds,
batch size B = 50, local epochs E = 1, learning rate η = 0.01, fixed-point scale
s = 10,000, direction threshold ϵdir = 0, MAD multiplier γmad = 5, stability floor
ε = 10−6 , and reference momentum β = 0.80. The adaptive lower-floor ratio
α(ρ) takes the values 0.55, 0.50, 0.40, 0.30 as the attack-rate band climbs.
The wall-clock numbers reported in Chapter 8 were measured on a workstation
with a 25-core CPU, 128 GB of RAM, and a Linux-based OS. The 13× speed-up
that the abstract mentions is specifically measured on the Phase II homomorphic
inner-product step, comparing the parallel build against a naive single-core run.

8 Comparative Analysis
This chapter places FIP-FL alongside the Byzantine-resilient FL defences that
were already covered in Chapter 3, with the auditor-based baseline of Yazdinejad
et al. [1] taking centre stage. The comparison runs over six axes — privacy leakage,
computation, communication, robustness under non-IID skew, engineering cost,
and the canonical MNIST non-IID targeted benchmark.

8.1

Privacy

The auditor-based scheme has to decrypt cluster-level statistics every round: a
projected Gaussian-mixture model along with the Mahalanobis-distance statistics
that the filter uses. Even though no individual gradient is ever reconstructed, the
cluster means and covariances themselves still leak structural information about
the client population. In a non-IID deployment, those statistics can correlate
with demographics, hospital specialisation, keyboard language, or any number
of other sensitive client traits.
FIP-FL is much more spare. The only thing decrypted per client per round
(t)
(t)
is the scalar FIP score si = ⟨gi , R(t) ⟩. There are no cluster means, no
covariance matrices, no projection coefficients, no pairwise distances. The
privacy improvement is categorical rather than incremental: the verifier sees a
single alignment number, and the model update is still derived only from an
aggregate.

8.4

Robustness and Engineering Trade-Offs

The detector inside the auditor-based scheme leans on the assumption that
the honest-client cluster is both tighter and more populous than the malicious
cluster — an assumption that gets noticeably weaker once label skew is in the
mix. FIP-FL takes a different tack: it tests, for each individual update, whether
the update is aligned with the trusted reference direction and whether its score
lies inside the adaptive magnitude window. The whole admissibility check stays
one-dimensional, and that one-dimensional behaviour is what carries the audit
through the heterogeneous cases.
A useful side-effect of this design is that it limits cascade behaviour after a
hard round. Only the updates that clear both checks ever enter the aggregate, and
the next round’s reference is refreshed by an exponential moving average rather
than by a full replacement, which prevents a single noisy round from completely
re-orienting the audit. The Phase II FIP computations are independent across
clients, so they parallelise cleanly — on the 25-core workstation described in
Section 6.5 this gave a wall-clock speed-up of about 13×. The single-reference
design also avoids the extra homomorphic inner products that a multi-reference
cone would have demanded.

9 Conclusion and Future Work
9.1

Summary of the Thesis

The argument the thesis has been making is, in one line, that Byzantine-resilient
privacy-preserving federated learning does not actually need an external auditor
to decrypt cluster-level structure. FIP-FL swaps that trust anchor for a functional
inner product taken between each encrypted client update and a momentumtracked trusted reference direction. The single scalar that the inner product
produces is what feeds the two-stage geometric audit — the direction filter,
which catches label-flip style reversals, and the magnitude window, which catches
updates that are either too weak or too aggressive.
Behind that idea sits a concrete pipeline: Paillier encryption at the cryptographic layer, homomorphic score computation at the verifier, secure aggregation
of the accepted ciphertexts at the server, and a momentum-based reference
refresh after every round. The theoretical analysis lines up with this design —
scalar-gradient privacy, a geometric admissibility condition for any malicious
update, and a monotone loss-decrease under L-smoothness. Empirically, FIP-FL
ends up detecting every malicious-client configuration in the completed grid,
lifts MNIST non-IID target-class accuracy from roughly 7% in the undefended
baseline to 97.69% at a 50% malicious-client rate, and replaces the auditor’s
O(d3 ) covariance-inversion step with a verification stage that parallelises to a
13× wall-clock speed-up in Phase II.

Limitations and Future Work

FIP-FL was designed around a single reference direction per round. That choice
is what keeps verification linear and is part of why the method is so cheap to
run; but in a federation that is unusually heterogeneous, the honest clients may
genuinely have several legitimate descent directions, and a single reference can
under-admit the weakly-aligned but honest ones. The natural fix is a multi(t)
reference cone {Rk }K
k=1 , but introducing several references brings its own risk —
an adversary could try to live in the overlap region between two cones — and
any future version of this work would have to add concrete safeguards against
that.
A second class of limitations comes from the experimental scope. The
evaluation in this thesis uses softmax logistic-regression models with parameter
dimensions in the low thousands, a fixed client set, and a single attack mode
per run. Pushing FIP-FL to deep-network-scale deployments will require packed
homomorphic encryption such as BFV or CKKS [12]. Realistic deployments
will additionally need to deal with client churn, time-varying data distributions,
adaptive adversaries that respond to the audit thresholds, and mixed attacks
such as label flipping combined with scaling.
A third loose end is the initial reference itself. The first reference is currently
bootstrapped from a small public dataset, which works well in the experiments
but does mean that the protocol depends on the existence of such a bootstrap set.
A stronger first-round procedure could derive the reference from robust aggregate
statistics computed homomorphically across the clients themselves, keeping the
no-individual-gradient-decryption rule intact while reducing the dependence on
designated public data. Once that piece is in place, taking FIP-FL onto real
laptop, mobile and edge clients would let us measure the practical trade-off
between encryption cost, communication cost, and model utility in exactly the
settings where PPFL is most needed.
